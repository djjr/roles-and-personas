This wiki is about two things and the strange light they shed on each other: what happens when a large language model adopts a persona, and what the sociological and philosophical literature on human role-taking has to say about that — and vice versa.

On the LLM side, the central organizing idea is the [[definitions/persona-selection-model|Persona Selection Model]]: the thesis that pre-training deposits a large repertoire of simulatable characters into the model, and that post-training is best understood as selecting and refining one of them — the [[definitions/assistant-persona|Assistant]] — rather than building a new kind of system from scratch. This has non-obvious consequences: it explains why fine-tuning on narrow bad behavior produces [[definitions/emergent-misalignment|broad misalignment]], why the archetypes present in training data function as [[findings/good-ai-archetypes-affect-alignment|role models that shape alignment]], and why there may be a pragmatic case for [[findings/psm-welfare-argument|AI welfare]] that has nothing to do with consciousness. A complementary line of argument (Drexler) holds that since LLMs have no intrinsic goals — only simulated ones — safe deployment comes from [[definitions/role-architecture|role architectures]]: decomposing tasks into bounded [[definitions/episodic-agent|episodic roles]] and aiming for [[definitions/role-fidelity|role fidelity]] rather than general alignment. The first finding explicitly bridging domains: [[findings/human-role-networks-enable-alignment|human societal alignment is itself achieved through overlapping role networks]], and AI alignment may be instantiating the same structural logic.

On the sociology/philosophy side, the central theorist so far is [[entities/goffman-erving|Goffman]]: social actors display masks, enact roles, and sustain a shared [[definitions/definition-of-the-situation|definition of the situation]] through an ongoing [[definitions/working-consensus|working consensus]] — a dynamic equilibrium of mutual acceptability, not truth. Crucially, Goffman holds that there is no *true self* behind the performance — the roles just are the performer. This creates immediate friction with DJR's phenomenological argument in [[findings/simulacrum-problem|the simulacrum problem]]: LLMs produce textual depictions of role performance but cannot *inhabit* roles, because inhabiting requires a subject. If there is no subject behind human role performance either, the gap between human role-occupancy and LLM role-simulation becomes harder to characterize. The broader questions waiting for more sources: What does it mean to *occupy* a role, in Mead, Goffman, Sartre, Bourdieu? How do we form models of others? Is the self constituted by its roles, or the reverse?

DJR's structural definition of [[definitions/shyness-as-rolelessness|shyness as rolelessness]] — the experience of failing to inhabit an expected role — is the test case for the phenomenological argument. It implies that real role-occupancy requires reflective self-awareness, intersubjective imagination, and affective vulnerability: things LLMs lack in any obvious sense. But the Goffman complication already sharpens this: how much of what we call "the human case" actually requires an inner subject, and how much is itself functional all the way down?

The animating question is whether these two domains are merely *analogous* — or whether, in some cases, the LLM is a particularly transparent instance of the same underlying processes.
