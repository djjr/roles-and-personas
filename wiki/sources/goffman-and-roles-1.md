## Précis

*Note: This is DJR's notes and marginalia on Goffman's* The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life *(1959), not a direct clip of the Goffman text. File appears to be a draft (ends mid-sentence on "Working con[sensus]").*

DJR's working notes on Goffman's foundational dramaturgical text. Key moves:

**Theatre metaphor with divergences.** Goffman uses theatre as a structural analogy for everyday interaction but the analogy is imperfect: in theatre there is a hard distinction between performance and reality, rehearsal vs. improvisation, and a dedicated audience who would not be present if the interaction were real. In everyday life these divisions are blurred.

**Roles as alignment mechanisms.** DJR frames Goffman's roles as "relatively low-dimensional bargain exchanges" — a framing that directly connects sociological role theory to multi-agent coordination. The Goffman quote that motivates this: "Information about the individual helps to define the situation, enabling others to know in advance what he will expect of them and what they may expect of him" (p. 1). DJR glosses: roles are scaffolding for a *generalized other* that the self uses to orient itself.

**The inference problem.** Most of what we know about others is not directly available; we interpolate and extrapolate. Goffman: "the 'true' or 'real' attitudes, beliefs, and emotions of the individual can be ascertained only indirectly, through his avowals or through what appears to be involuntary expressive behaviour" (pp. 1–2). In the co-presence of others, "his activity will have a promissory character" (p. 2).

**Definition of the situation as a lowest-energy state.** DJR: agents actively try to impose *their* definition of the situation — to get others to act in accordance with their plans. DJR's original reframing: the actual definition of the situation is likely "the lowest energy state that is compatible with what all the agents are seeing and seeing that others are seeing." Cf. Goffman's *working consensus*.

**Working consensus.** Goffman: "each participant is expected to suppress his immediate heartfelt feelings, conveying a view of the situation which he feels the others will be able to find at least temporarily acceptable" (p. 4). This is the dynamic equilibrium underlying stable interaction — not truth or sincerity but mutual acceptability.

## DJR perspective

The core insight DJR is developing here is that **roles + definition of the situation = a coordination technology** — a low-dimensional compressed representation that allows agents to act on reliable expectations about each other without needing full mutual transparency. This maps cleanly onto alignment concerns: if you know someone is acting in the role of "doctor," you can predict a large structured space of their behavior without having to model their full psychology. DJR also flags: "how much abstraction can we apply?" — the compression is useful precisely because it discards detail; the question is what gets lost.

The inference problem note is analytically important. Goffman's point — that we infer inner states from behavior, never accessing them directly — is structurally identical to the ELK problem for LLMs: we can only read off outputs, never the underlying representations. DJR does not make this connection explicitly in these notes but it is latent.

DJR's "lowest energy state" framing of the definition of the situation is original and precise. It implies: (1) the working definition is not imposed by any single agent but emerges from the interaction; (2) it is the result of all agents' simultaneous attempts to impose their preferred frame; (3) the stable definition is the one that minimizes conflict among those attempts. This is a micro-sociological equilibrium model.

## Tensions / contradictions with existing wiki

- **Inference problem ↔ ELK**: Goffman's indirect-access claim about inner states parallels the ELK problem for LLMs. Should be connected once ELK source page is developed beyond stub.
- **Roles as alignment mechanisms ↔ Role Architectures (Drexler)**: Both Goffman (1959) and Drexler (2023) arrive at the idea that roles constrain behavior in ways useful for coordination. Goffman's version is sociological (roles enable mutual expectation); Drexler's is technical (role fidelity enables safe delegation). The convergence is worth marking.
- **Working consensus ↔ Echoing**: In human interaction the working consensus is maintained through continuous mutual monitoring and adjustment. In AxA LLM interaction this mechanism is absent — the agents cannot genuinely negotiate a shared frame. This deepens the [[definitions/echoing]] diagnosis.
