## Précis

*Source type: YouTube embed with transcript clip, sourced from BBC Open University. Original video: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6Z0XS-QLDWM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6Z0XS-QLDWM)*

Short pedagogical presentation of Goffman's dramaturgical sociology, framed by Shakespeare's "all the world's a stage." Core claims:

1. **Mask display**: We display a series of masks to others — enacting roles, controlling and staging how we appear, concerned with how we are coming across.
2. **Situation-governed role selection**: We play different parts determined by the situations we take ourselves to be in and how we think we are coming across; we adapt what we are depending on who we are interacting with.
3. **Role conflict in awkward situations**: The dramaturgical model is most visible when we find ourselves juggling two inconsistent roles simultaneously — e.g., encountering friends from incompatible social groups.
4. **No true self**: The analogy with acting only goes so far: for Goffman, there is no true self, no identifiable performer *behind* the role. The roles just *are* the performer. This challenges the idea of a fixed character or psychological identity.
