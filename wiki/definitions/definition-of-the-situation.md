The **definition of the situation** is the shared, mutually recognized normative frame within which social interaction takes place. The concept originates with W.I. Thomas ("If men define situations as real, they are real in their consequences" — the *Thomas theorem*) and is central to Goffman's dramaturgical sociology: before a performance can begin, participants must establish — tacitly or explicitly — what kind of encounter this is, what roles are in play, and what normative expectations govern it.

Goffman's own formulation: "Information about the individual helps to define the situation, enabling others to know in advance what he will expect of them and what they may expect of him" (*Presentation of Self in Everyday Life*, 1959, p. 1). The definition of the situation is thus partly constituted by role information — knowing someone's role is a large part of knowing what situation you are in. DJR glosses this as roles being scaffolding for a *generalized other* that the self can use to orient itself.

Crucially, the definition of the situation is *common knowledge* in the technical sense: each participant not only knows the frame, but knows that others know it, and knows that others know they know, and so on. This mutual recognition is what enables ongoing role reinforcement — each participant's compliance with the frame is simultaneously a confirmation that the frame holds. The dynamic equilibrium that sustains this is Goffman's [[definitions/working-consensus|working consensus]].

DJR's original framing: the working definition of the situation is likely "the lowest energy state that is compatible with what all the agents are seeing and seeing that others are seeing" — it is emergent, not dictated, arising from the simultaneous attempts of all participants to impose their preferred frame.

In this wiki, the concept is first introduced via DJR's analysis of the [[definitions/echoing|echoing]] failure: LLM agents in AxA interactions lack the shared situation-construction that human role-players continuously perform, which is why their roles drift. The system prompt is a unilateral role assignment, not a mutually negotiated frame.

*Analogy target:* On the LLM side, role stability requires more than a role definition — it requires something analogous to the interactive establishment and maintenance of a shared frame. The absence of this in current LLM architectures is a precise structural gap, not merely a behavioral observation.
