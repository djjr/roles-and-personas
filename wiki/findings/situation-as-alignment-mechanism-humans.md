## Claim

Human role performance in interaction is stabilized not by the role definition alone but by the continuous mutual construction and affirmation of a shared definition of the situation — a normative frame each participant holds as common knowledge. LLM agents lack this mechanism, which is why their roles drift.

## Evidence

- [[sources/echoing-identity-failures]]: DJR analysis. The role definition in a system prompt is a unilateral assignment; it is not mutually negotiated or interactively reinforced. Human interactants continuously signal that the other's performance is legible as role-appropriate, which keeps the frame alive. "Situation, then, is itself an alignment mechanism for human intelligences" (DJR).

## Contradicted by / Tension with

- This is a theoretical claim extended from the empirical echoing result — not directly tested in the paper. Whether interactive situation-construction could be replicated in AxA systems (e.g., via explicit shared frame protocols) is an open engineering question.
