## Claim

Human societal alignment — the constraint of individual behavior toward collectively tolerable outcomes — is largely achieved through participation in overlapping, mutually constraining role networks; role architecture for AI may be instantiating the same structural logic.

## Evidence

- [[sources/role-architectures-drexler]]: DJR note explicit: "Human alignment is largely made possible because human societies are a giant set of overlapping networks of roles." Drexler's role-architecture proposal, on this reading, is importing that social-structural solution into AI deployment. Supporting observation: "rolelessness has alignment implications at both ends of the spectrum" — macro-actors without role constraints (tyrants) and micro-actors without role structures (pathological withdrawal) both represent alignment failures, though DJR notes the tyrant case is complicated by tyranny itself being a kind of role.

## Contradicted by / Tension with

- The human case involves roles that are internalized, contested, and dynamically renegotiated — not just architecturally assigned. Whether role fidelity can be achieved in LLMs without the internalization that makes human role-playing robust is an open question.
- Drexler himself doesn't make the sociological argument explicitly; this is DJR's extension. The analogy is suggestive but needs to be tested as sociology sources come into the wiki.
