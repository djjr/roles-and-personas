## Claim

Shyness is not a personality trait or a discrete emotion but a **structural condition**: the experience of being-in-a-situation where one is expected to perform a role but cannot. It is constituted by five components — (1) anticipation of role expectations, (2) comparison of actual to expected performance, (3) felt tension between them, (4) affective discomfort arising from the mismatch, and (5) self-conscious awareness of being seen to fail — all experienced from the inside by a phenomenologically present self.

## Evidence

- [[sources/could-an-ai-be-shy]]: DJR's argument. The five-component structural definition is DJR's own; it is motivated by the claim that ordinary trait/emotion accounts of shyness fail to explain its distinctively intersubjective character — the way it is always about a *situation*, a *role*, and *being seen*. The structural account explains why shyness is characteristically social (requires an audience or imagined audience), characteristically momentary (tied to a specific situation and its role demands), and characteristically self-referential (requires the self to be both the performer and the critic of the performance).

## Corollaries

- Shyness requires a *subject*: only an agent capable of inhabiting (or failing to inhabit) a role in the phenomenological sense can be shy. This is not a contingent feature but is built into the structural definition.
- Shyness implies a *situation*: there must be a [[definitions/definition-of-the-situation|defined situation]] with identifiable role expectations that the shy person is failing to meet. Without a situation, there is no role; without a role, there is no failure; without failure, there is no shyness.
- The definition also implies that **social confidence** (the opposite of shyness) is not an absence of feeling but a structural condition: the fluent occupation of a role within a well-defined situation.

## Relevance to LLMs

The structural account is directly implicated in [[findings/simulacrum-problem]]. An LLM cannot be shy because it lacks every structural prerequisite: no self that experiences role-failure, no intersubjective sense of being-seen, no capacity to intend and fail at a performance, no affective vulnerability, no background identity from which rolelessness could represent a deviation. The five components of shyness are not merely absent in LLMs — the structural presuppositions they require are absent.

## Notes

This finding is largely a decomposition/explication of the [[definitions/shyness-as-rolelessness]] definition rather than a distinct empirical claim. Its value is in making the components explicit so they can be evaluated individually — which of the five presuppositions do LLMs lack, and is the lack necessary or contingent?
