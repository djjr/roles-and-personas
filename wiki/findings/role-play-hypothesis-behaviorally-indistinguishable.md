## Claim

The role play hypothesis — that LLMs "act as if" they have beliefs and desires without really having them — makes no behavioral predictions distinct from the belief/desire hypothesis; if so, the dispute is verbal, and role play is at least as anthropomorphic as direct belief/desire attribution.

## Evidence

- [Goldstein & Lederman 2025]: Role play (Shanahan et al. 2023, Janus 2022, Mitchell 2024) is offered as a way to use folk-psychological language without "anthropomorphism." G&L's response: (1) belief/desire attribution is not anthropomorphic — bats and insects have beliefs and desires; (2) role play is *more* anthropomorphic, since arguably only humans engage in genuine pretense; (3) even Shanahan et al. slip into agentic language ("the model will do its best to role-play…"). → [[sources/goldstein-lederman-2025-what-does-chatgpt-want]]
- Deeper challenge: if role play makes no distinct behavioral predictions, then by symmetry the "act as if" move applies equally to humans — a conclusion almost no one accepts. Proponents of role play owe an account of why the genuine article is attributed to humans but not LLMs; the most natural answer invokes phenomenal consciousness, but that is then doing all the work, not role play.

## Contradicted by / Tension with

- [[findings/simulacrum-problem]]: DJR's simulacrum argument holds that LLMs produce role-outputs without inhabiting the role — the output is structurally isomorphic but the underlying subject is absent. G&L's challenge to DJR: this argument either (a) covertly requires phenomenal consciousness (in which case it should be stated that way), or (b) collapses into the verbal dispute they identify. The simulacrum-problem finding survives only if grounded in phenomenological commitments G&L explicitly bracket.

## Notes

G&L note the role play hypothesis is most natural as a hypothesis about the *model agent*, not the instance agent. Since they reject the model agent, this further undercuts role play's purchase. This finding is among the most significant for the wiki's central tension: it reframes the simulacrum debate as either a consciousness debate in disguise or a verbal dispute.
