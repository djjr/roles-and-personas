[Erving Goffman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erving_Goffman) (1922–1982), Canadian-American sociologist. Principal theorist of **dramaturgical sociology** — the analysis of everyday social interaction through the metaphor of theatrical performance. Major work: *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959).

Core theoretical moves relevant to this wiki:

- **Theatre as structural analogy**: Social actors display masks, enact roles, and manage impressions in a manner structurally analogous to theatrical performance, though with important disanalogies (no fixed audience/performer distinction, no script, no separation of stage from life).
- **No true self**: For Goffman, there is no performer behind the mask — the roles *are* the performer. This challenges the notion of a fixed psychological identity beneath the performance.
- **Definition of the situation**: Interaction requires participants to establish a shared normative frame — what kind of encounter this is, what roles are in play. See [[definitions/definition-of-the-situation]].
- **Working consensus**: Stable interaction is maintained through a dynamic equilibrium of mutual acceptability, not through truth or full sincerity. See [[definitions/working-consensus]].
- **The inference problem**: Inner states are only ever accessible indirectly through behavior and avowals; interaction is fundamentally built on inference, not transparency.

**DJR reading context**: Goffman is being read as a sociological source for the role-theory side of the wiki's bidirectional analogy. The alignment-relevant connections are: (1) roles as coordination technology (low-dimensional compressed expectations enabling multi-agent cooperation); (2) the definition of the situation as a mechanism explaining role stability and drift; (3) the no-true-self claim as a potential complication for DJR's phenomenological arguments in [[findings/simulacrum-problem]].
